Andhra Pradesh – Pootharekulu

Pootharekulu is a paper-thin sweet made with rice starch, ghee, and jaggery or sugar. Originating from Atreyapuram, it's a traditional Andhra delicacy that melts in the mouth. The delicate sheets wrap around a sweet filling, offering a delightful balance of crisp texture and rich, sugary flavor.

Arunachal Pradesh – Thukpa

Thukpa is a hearty noodle soup made with vegetables or meat, originating from the Tibetan-influenced areas of Arunachal. It's a comforting and nourishing dish, typically flavored with local herbs and spices.

Assam – Masor Tenga

Masor Tenga is a light and tangy Assamese fish curry made with tomatoes, lemon, or elephant apple. It's typically cooked with freshwater fish and served with rice. This dish showcases Assam's love for mild, sour flavors and its deep connection to fish and seasonal local produce.

Bihar – Litti Chokha

Litti Chokha is a traditional Bihari dish consisting of wheat balls stuffed with sattu (roasted gram flour) served with mashed vegetables like brinjal, potato, and tomato. Roasted over fire and drizzled with ghee, it's rustic, nutritious, and reflects Bihar's earthy, agrarian food culture.

Chhattisgarh – Faraa

Faraa are steamed dumplings made of rice flour, often stuffed or spiced with local herbs. They are healthy and light, traditionally enjoyed as breakfast or snacks. Faraa is a staple in Chhattisgarh households and showcases the state's preference for simple, rice-based, home-cooked meals.

Gujarat – Dhokla

Dhokla is a savory steamed cake made from fermented rice and chickpea flour. Soft, spongy, and slightly tangy, it's a beloved Gujarati snack often served with green chutney. Dhokla reflects Gujarat's vegetarian culinary heritage and is commonly enjoyed during breakfast or festive occasions.

Jharkhand – Thekua

Thekua is a deep-fried sweet snack made from whole wheat flour, jaggery, and ghee. It's especially prepared during the Chhath festival and holds cultural significance in Jharkhand and Bihar. Crisp outside and soft inside, Thekua is a symbol of tradition, simplicity, and devotion.

Kerala – Unniyappam

Unniyappam are small, sweet rice flour fritters made with jaggery, banana, and coconut. Deep-fried to golden brown, they're soft and spongy, traditionally offered in temples. This beloved snack is a symbol of Kerala's love for coconut, rice, and sweets infused with local flavors.

Madhya Pradesh – Bhutte ka Kees

Bhutte ka Kees is a street-style delicacy made from grated corn cooked with spices, milk, and ghee. Originating in Indore, it's creamy, mildly sweet, and aromatic. This dish showcases how simple ingredients like corn can be transformed into a unique, flavorful regional specialty.

Maharashtra – Misal Pav

Cheese Pav Bhaji is a Mumbai street food made by mashing spiced vegetables into a buttery curry, topped with cheese and served with buttered pav. It's a modern twist on a traditional favorite and represents Maharashtra's love for spicy, rich, and indulgent street-style meals

Manipur – Eromba

Eromba is a traditional Manipuri dish made with mashed boiled vegetables, fermented fish (ngari), and chili. It's intensely flavorful and often eaten with rice. Eromba reflects the state's reliance on fermentation and bold flavors, rooted in indigenous cooking traditions.

Meghalaya – Jadoh

Jadoh is a Khasi rice dish cooked with pork, turmeric, and aromatic spices. It's a hearty and savory meal often enjoyed during celebrations. The name literally means 'rice and meat,' showcasing Meghalaya's tribal food culture centered on meat and indigenous ingredients.

Mizoram – Bai

Bai is a healthy Mizo dish made with steamed vegetables, bamboo shoot, and fermented pork. Mild in spices, it's typically eaten with rice. Bai reflects the Mizoram way of life-simple, nutritious, and closely tied to locally available herbs and seasonal produce.

Indian Regional Food

Indian food is more than just a meal,it's an emotion, a tradition, and a celebration served on a plate. From the smoky clay ovens of the north to the sizzling iron tawas of the south, every corner of India simmers with a unique culinary rhythm. It's a country where recipes are passed down like heirlooms, where every spice has a purpose, and where food is both sacred and sensational. Step into any Indian kitchen and you're greeted by the crackle of mustard seeds in oil, the scent of curry leaves, the swirl of turmeric in the air. The diversity is breathtaking—yet there's a beautiful harmony in the chaos of flavors. One state sweetens its dal, another spikes it with chili, and both claim it as comfort food. This is the magic of India: a land where no two kitchens cook the same way, yet every meal feels like home. Here, a flatbread isn't just a flatbread, it's phulka, roti, paratha, puri, bhakri, or appam, depending on where you are. Rice isn't simply rice; it's biryani, pulao, khichdi, pongal, or sticky rice wrapped in banana leaves. And the love for pickles? Unites us all. Whether it's fiery mango achaar in the north or tangy fish pickles in the coastal belts, a jar of pickle holds memories of sun-dried summers and grandma's touch. Food in India tells stories of festivals lit by laddoos and barfis, of weddings crowned with elaborate thalis, of street corners echoing with the sizzle of chaats and the aroma of pakoras during monsoons. Every dish carries a whisper of the land it comes from: the rugged deserts, the lush coasts, the misty hills, the fertile plains.
